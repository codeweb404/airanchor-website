AirAnchor

Subtitle

Air anchor is a revolutionary product which aids the field of meteorological research by remedying two of its iconic and crucial problems: aircraft drift and lack of reusability.

During ascent, the air anchor utilizes its four lightweight kevlar flaps to convert horizontal forces, created by the wind, into rotational force, effectively reducing its overall drift distance by 50%.

Once the air anchor reaches its desired altitude, it uses a sophisticated pulley-motor system to change its center of mass, effectively rotating the entire structure. As it descends slowly, the stepper motors, gps and wind detection sensors of the AirAnchor work in harmony to control and coordinate the movement of the now vertically rotating flaps. The asymmetry created by this operation coupled with the magnus effect of the falling balloon allows the AirAnchor to fully coordinate and control the direction and trajectory of its descent and landing.

Subtitle

At higher altitudes in the troposphere wind speeds can exceed over 100 knots. This can cause typical meteorological devices, such as radiosonde balloons, to drift up to 66 km horizontally before reaching their goal of 20 km above sea level. AirAnchor reduces this drift by 50% though its rotational mechanism.

Kevlar 49 or HM kevlar is one of DuPont’s flagship ARAMID fibers. With its incomparable low-temperature resistance, extreme integrity and light weight, it allows the AirAnchor’s flaps to maintain integrity and catch wind even under the treacherous conditions of high altitudes.

As atmospheric pressure drops with rising altitude, the helium balloon expands in both diameter and volume. To accommodate for this increase, the AirAnchor employs a complaint chain mechanism made with a thin sheet of titanium. The crisscrossing patterns allow for the chain to be stretched without damaging the balloon inside, while also enabling it to return to its form once it reaches sea level.

The double bearing connection module below the AirAnchor allows for the complete isolation of the measurement module, ensuring that the volatile rotations of the AirAnchor will not affect the precision of the measurements conducted.

Subtitle

Rotating a 2m wide structure mid-air can seem like a daunting task. However, for the AirAnchor, its cleverly designed pulley-motor system executes this task effortlessly. Once the altimeter reads that the AirAnchor has reached its target height, the pulley-motor system unravels the kevlar cord and displaces the measurement device, relocating the entire system's center of mass - effectively rotating the AirAnchor.

Subtitle

Even with the use of durable kevlar sheets and cords, the harsh winds below the stratosphere pose a challenge for controlling the flaps. In order to supply sufficient torque for controlling the flaps, the AirAnchor pairs a relatively small stepper motor with a robust gearbox. This gearbox allows the AirAnchor’s small stepper motors to output a massive amount of torque and force, creating slow yet undefiable changes in the flap’s rotation.
